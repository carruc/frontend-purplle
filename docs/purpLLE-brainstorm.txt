MIT fireside chat with Mitch Resnick (20 min): 
   * When considering new technologies like AI, the primary question should be What type of learning and education do we want for our children and society?
   * Resnick contrasts the instructionist approach (using tech to deliver instruction more efficiently) with the constructionist approach (using tech to engage learners in designing, creating, and experimenting on projects based on their interests)
   * Resnick sees generative AI as having greater potential than previous AI forms to support either the instructionist or constructionist approach, offering more choices
   * He expresses discouragement that much of the current EdTech world is using generative AI primarily to reinforce traditional, instructionist approaches, rather than fostering needed changes towards more creative, student-driven learning: THIS IS IMPORTANT. young people to develop creative thinking, curiosity, and collaborative skills to navigate an unpredictable future.
   * AI should be almost invisible. Not even mentioned. It should be an enabler, not a protagonist
   * If an assignment (like writing) can be easily completed by AI (like ChatGPT), it may indicate a need to design assignments that require more personal connection, reflection, and critical thinking-tasks AI cannot replicate authentically
   * This AI debate mirrors older debates such as the interne, social media, etcetera.
   * AI can be framed as another resource (like Google or YouTube) that learners can control and use when working on projects, helping them find information or overcome obstacles. We want to give them a pretty direct experience, avoiding analysis paralysis.
   * Passive vs. Active Role: The student's role within the app's core loop seems relatively passive during the "testing" phase – they consume questions generated by the AI. The active learning ("study independently") happens outside the app's direct interactive environment. Resnick advocates for technology that enables active doing and making within the tool itself.
   * Emphasis on Learner Agency (Nuance): Resnick might acknowledge that personalized study and testing modalities could contribute to learner agency, if implemented thoughtfully. The search results support the idea that personalized feedback, in particular, can enhance self-regulated learning and motivation. If the app allows students to truly customize how they are tested and receive feedback in ways that match their individual learning styles and preferences, that could be a positive.
   * Review of our idea given these parameters.

Design thinking approach:
Step 0: Find the problem
I'm interested in the fundamental challenges of humans in engaging in learning—but in order to identify the ones which are useful to me, I'd like to use a top-down approach. In other words, I'd like to understand the current system/environment for learners, and then start questioning which problems arise and why. Right now, my thoughts are directed at Italian university students. The university system in Italy usually features two semesters, each of these organized in a lessons period of around four months, and an exam session of around 1 month and a half. Students should attend "passive" (traditional) lessons for six different exams at the same time during the lessons period, and then sit 6 exams with different modalities during the exams session—on top of possible final projects assigned by professors. My assumption is that this system is almost the opposite to one that truly supports and teaches to be a lifelong learner; which would implement structured continuous assessment, active learning, projects, and in general test and aim at improving all facets of competence: memory, understanding, application, exploration—knowledge, know-how, and creation of knowledge. I'd be super interested in changing the system, but it is not what I'm exploring right now—as there are many social, economical, structural barriers that keep this system as described. Most students do not enjoy this university system, finding it less than ideal (even though we do have extensive literature on the matter, we have not changed it to accommodate human-learning, in all its expressions). This often leads to studying "for the exam" instead of viewing the act of studying as a component to learning and improvement: lessons are skipped, choosing to study on slides or video-lessons; cramming is almost incentivized, and long-term retention is not. We are not robots; learning shouldn't be our only obsession, but we need it to adapt; and attending an university without learning, and learning to learn, is exceptionally useless. Students don't identify as learners, but as students. And learning is not a problem limited to academic campuses: in a constantly changing world, the art of learning is our most important endeavour. We could argue that the creation, exploration and communication of knowledge and know-how are the key differentiators for humans as a species: we are actively creating new chaos and new order from it.
